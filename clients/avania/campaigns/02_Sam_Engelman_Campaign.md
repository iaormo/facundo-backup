**Email Body:**

Hi [First Name],

Digital health 510(k)s have a problem: everyone thinks the algorithm is the hardest part. It's not.

The hard part is **predicate selection for software**, demonstrating substantial equivalence when your technology is fundamentally different, and building clinical evidence that FDA finds credible for a novel digital approach.

I'm Sam Engelman, and I specialize in digital health regulatory strategy at Avania Clinical. Before joining Avania, I spent years at MCRA navigating SaMD pathways, and I've seen the same mistakes repeatedly:

**Where digital health 510(k) strategies go wrong:**

→ **Predicate selection:** Choosing a device based on intended use but missing technological differences FDA considers non-equivalent
→ **Clinical evidence gaps:** Assuming analytical validation of your algorithm = clinical validation in FDA's eyes (it doesn't)
→ **Cybersecurity documentation:** Treating cybersecurity as a separate annex instead of integrated regulatory architecture
→ **Software level of concern:** Underestimating your software's risk classification and FDA's documentation expectations

The companies that get digital health 510(k)s cleared in 6-9 months do three things differently:
1. They choose predicates FDA will actually accept (not just the closest technology)
2. They generate clinical evidence that satisfies both clearance and reimbursement needs
3. They document software validation at the level of concern FDA assigns (not what they hope for)

**What a digital health 510(k) strategy engagement delivers:**
- Comprehensive predicate analysis with FDA acceptance probability assessment
- 510(k) vs. De Novo pathway recommendation based on predicate landscape
- Clinical evidence strategy: what studies FDA needs, what can be real-world data
- Software validation documentation aligned with your device's level of concern
- Cybersecurity and electrical testing documentation architecture

If you're planning a 510(k) submission for a software-based device, getting the strategy right before submission saves 6-12 months on the back end.

Let's talk through your pathway options. 30 minutes this week?

## EXECUTION STRATEGY

### Why Qualify-First Approach?

The tactical sequences above use a **"qualify first, no call ask"** approach in early touches. This differs from the longer value-proposition emails in the strategic section.

**When to Use Each:**

**Qualify-First Tactical Sequences** (from PDF):
- **Best for:** High-volume prospecting (100+ contacts per slice)
- **Goal:** Fast qualification and filtering
- **Tone:** Concise, conversational, low pressure
- **Response optimization:** Higher response rates from brevity
- **Use when:** Building pipeline, testing new lists, broad outreach

**Value-Proposition Strategic Emails** (from earlier in document):
- **Best for:** Targeted outreach to known high-value prospects
- **Goal:** Establish expertise and credibility upfront
- **Tone:** Thought leadership, technical depth
- **Response optimization:** Lower volume, higher quality conversations
- **Use when:** Named accounts, referrals, warm introductions, conference follow-ups

### Recommended Approach

**Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Qualify-First**
- Use tactical sequences for initial outreach to cold lists
- Focus on fast qualification: wearable vs. algorithm, planning stage, right person
- Goal: 5-10% response rate, 20-30 qualified conversations

**Phase 2 (Weeks 5-8): Value-Proposition**
- Use strategic emails for non-responders who look like strong fits
- Demonstrate technical expertise with PCCP, SaMD, cardiovascular details
- Goal: 2-5% response rate from previously cold prospects

**Ongoing: Hybrid**
- Qualify-first for new cold lists
- Value-proposition for referrals, event follow-ups, re-engagement
- Match message complexity to prospect familiarity
